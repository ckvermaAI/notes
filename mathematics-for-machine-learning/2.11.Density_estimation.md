*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 11*

## 11.1 Gaussian Mixture Model

A GMM represents $p(\boldsymbol{x})$ as a convex combination of $K$ Gaussians:
$$p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k\,\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$$

with $\pi_k \geq 0$, $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$, and $\boldsymbol{\theta} = \{\pi_k, \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k\}_{k=1}^K$.

Single Gaussians cannot capture multimodal distributions. GMMs are **universal density approximators** (in the limit of $K$) for smooth densities. Unlike histograms or KDE, GMMs are parametric and generative.

> **Intuition.** A GMM models the data as coming from $K$ latent "clusters," each Gaussian-distributed. The mixing weights $\pi_k$ give the prior probability of a point belonging to cluster $k$. The universality follows from the fact that any smooth density can be approximated arbitrarily well by a sum of Gaussians — this is the same as saying Gaussians form a basis for the space of densities in the same way polynomials form a basis for the space of functions. In practice, $K$ is small (2–20) and each component covers a coherent region of the data.

> **Usage.** Density estimation (estimating $p(\boldsymbol{x})$ from data), soft clustering (posterior responsibilities $r_{nk}$ give cluster memberships), generative modeling (sample new data by ancestral sampling), anomaly detection (points with low $p(\boldsymbol{x})$ under the fitted GMM are anomalies), and as a prior/likelihood in Bayesian models.

> **Practicality.** The covariance structure of each component is a modeling choice: full covariance ($D(D+1)/2$ parameters), diagonal (assumes independence within cluster, $D$ parameters), spherical (isotropic, 1 parameter). Use full covariance when clusters have arbitrary shape; use diagonal for high-dimensional data (fewer parameters, less risk of singularity). With $D$ large and $N$ moderate, the $K \times D \times D$ covariance matrices can overfit — regularize by adding $\epsilon\boldsymbol{I}$ or using diagonal/tied covariances.

## 11.2 Parameter Learning via Maximum Likelihood

**Log-likelihood**:
$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{n=1}^N \log\sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k\,\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$$

The log-sum structure prevents closed-form MLE (gradient equations become coupled through the **responsibilities**):
$$r_{nk} = \frac{\pi_k\,\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_j \pi_j\,\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)} = p(z_k = 1 \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n)$$

Setting gradients to zero yields update equations (used in the M-step):
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^\text{new} = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk}\boldsymbol{x}_n}{\sum_n r_{nk}} = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk}\boldsymbol{x}_n}{N_k}$$
$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^\text{new} = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_n r_{nk}(\boldsymbol{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)(\boldsymbol{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T$$
$$\pi_k^\text{new} = \frac{N_k}{N}, \quad N_k = \sum_n r_{nk}$$

These are **responsibility-weighted** statistics: each data point contributes to each component proportionally to its posterior probability of belonging to it.

> **Intuition.** The log-likelihood is a log of a sum — you cannot push the log inside because of the mixture structure. If you knew which component each point came from (i.e., if $\boldsymbol{z}_n$ were observed), the MLE would be trivial (just fit $K$ separate Gaussians). The EM algorithm resolves this by computing expected cluster assignments ("soft" assignments) and then fitting as if those expectations were the true assignments. The $r_{nk}$ are the fractional memberships that emerge from Bayes' rule.

> **Usage.** The update equations reveal the structure of EM for all latent variable models: the M-step always takes responsibility-weighted averages of sufficient statistics. For Gaussian components, the sufficient statistics are the mean and covariance, so the M-step is just a weighted mean and weighted covariance computation.

> **Practicality.** Log-likelihood computation must be done in log-space for numerical stability: use `scipy.special.logsumexp` or `torch.logsumexp` to compute $\log\sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$ without underflow. The responsibilities $r_{nk}$ should also be computed in log-space and then exponentiated. `sklearn.mixture.GaussianMixture` handles all this correctly.

## 11.3 EM Algorithm

**Expectation Maximization** alternates between soft assignments and parameter updates:

1. **Initialize** $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k, \pi_k$ (e.g., K-means++ seeds).
2. **E-step**: compute responsibilities $r_{nk}$ given current $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.
3. **M-step**: update $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ using the closed-form equations above.
4. **Repeat** until convergence (e.g., $|\Delta\mathcal{L}| < \epsilon$ or $\|\Delta\boldsymbol{\theta}\| < \epsilon$).

**Guarantee**: each EM iteration is guaranteed to non-decrease $\mathcal{L}$ (Neal & Hinton, 1999). This follows from the ELBO decomposition: $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{Q}(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) + \text{const}$ where $\mathcal{Q}$ is the expected complete-data log-likelihood; the M-step maximizes $\mathcal{Q}$.

**Caveats**: EM converges to a local optimum; sensitive to initialization. Multiple random restarts and K-means++ initialization mitigate this. Can be slow to converge near saddle points (especially when components overlap heavily).

> **Intuition.** EM is coordinate ascent on the ELBO with respect to the variational distribution $q(\boldsymbol{z})$ (E-step: set $q(\boldsymbol{z}) = p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$) and the model parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ (M-step: maximize $\mathbb{E}_{q}[\log p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})]$). The monotone convergence guarantee comes from Jensen's inequality: the ELBO is a lower bound on the log-likelihood, and each E/M step tightens or improves it. The algorithm is guaranteed to climb the likelihood surface, never descend — but it may get stuck at a local maximum.

> **Usage.** EM generalizes beyond GMMs to any model with latent variables where the posterior $p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ is tractable: HMMs (Baum-Welch = EM for HMMs), factor analysis, PPCA, missing data imputation, mixture of experts. Variational EM (VEM) replaces the exact E-step with an approximate $q(\boldsymbol{z})$ (mean-field, etc.) for intractable posteriors.

> **Practicality.** K-means++ initialization (assign initial $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ using K-means++ seeding) is critical — random initialization frequently leads to poor local optima where multiple components collapse onto the same cluster. Run EM 10–20 times with different initializations and pick the run with the highest final log-likelihood. Convergence check: monitor $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)}) - \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) < \epsilon$ (e.g., $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$). A decrease (even tiny) indicates a numerical bug, since EM is guaranteed to non-decrease.

## 11.4 Latent Variable Perspective

Introduce a one-hot latent vector $\boldsymbol{z} = [z_1, \ldots, z_K]^T$ with $p(z_k = 1) = \pi_k$:

**Generative process**:
1. Sample $\boldsymbol{z} \sim \text{Categorical}(\boldsymbol{\pi})$
2. Sample $\boldsymbol{x} \mid z_k = 1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$

This is **ancestral sampling**. Marginalizing over $\boldsymbol{z}$ recovers the GMM mixture density.

**Posterior** $p(z_k = 1 \mid \boldsymbol{x}) = r_{nk}$: the E-step computes exactly this via Bayes' theorem.

**General EM view** (ELBO):
- E-step maximizes the ELBO over $q(\boldsymbol{z})$ (for GMMs, the exact posterior is tractable)
- M-step maximizes the expected complete-data log-likelihood $\mathcal{Q}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}}[\log p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})]$
- For intractable posteriors, variational EM approximates $q(\boldsymbol{z}) \approx p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x})$

> **Intuition.** The complete-data log-likelihood $\log p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ is easy to maximize because with $\boldsymbol{z}$ known, the problem decomposes into $K$ independent Gaussian fitting problems. EM avoids the hard combinatorial assignment problem ($z_k \in \{0,1\}$) by using soft assignments — it maximizes the *expected* complete-data log-likelihood where the expectation averages over all possible assignments weighted by their posterior probability. This is the key algorithmic insight of EM.

> **Usage.** The ELBO = $\mathcal{Q}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + H[q]$ (expected complete-data log-likelihood + entropy of $q$). When $q = p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ (exact posterior, as in GMM), the ELBO equals the log-marginal-likelihood. When $q$ is a variational approximation, the ELBO is a lower bound — this is the training objective in VAEs. The EM/VEM connection unifies GMMs, HMMs, PPCA, VAEs, and Boltzmann machines under a single framework.

> **Practicality.** The ancestral sampling recipe (sample $k$ from the categorical, then $\boldsymbol{x}$ from component $k$) gives an $O(KD^2)$-per-sample generative model — trivially parallelizable. For anomaly detection: compute $\log p(\boldsymbol{x})$ for each test point; flag those below a threshold. For density estimation evaluation: use log-likelihood on a held-out test set as the primary metric.

## 11.5 Further Reading

**GMM vs K-means**: K-means is EM with hard assignments ($r_{nk} \in \{0,1\}$) and isotropic, equal-variance clusters. GMMs with hard assignments in the limit $\sigma^2 \to 0$ recover K-means.

**Selecting $K$**: BIC ($-2\mathcal{L} + k\log N$ where $k$ is parameter count), AIC ($-2\mathcal{L} + 2k$), or marginal likelihood (Bayesian). Nested cross-validation is an alternative.

**Limitations of MLE for GMMs**:
- **Degenerate solutions**: a component can collapse onto a single data point ($\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k \to 0$, $\mathcal{L} \to \infty$). Regularization (add $\epsilon\boldsymbol{I}$ to $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$) or Bayesian priors prevent this.
- **No uncertainty quantification**: Bayesian GMMs with Dirichlet priors on $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ and Normal-Wishart priors on $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$ give full posteriors; inference via variational EM or Gibbs sampling.

**Alternative density estimators**:
- **KDE**: $p(\boldsymbol{x}) = \frac{1}{Nh}\sum_n k\!\left(\frac{\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{x}_n}{h}\right)$ — nonparametric, no training, bandwidth $h$ via cross-validation. Scales poorly with $N$ and $D$.
- **Normalizing flows**: learned invertible transformations mapping a simple distribution to the data distribution; exact likelihood, scalable.
- **Dirichlet Process GMM**: nonparametric Bayesian GMM with automatically inferred $K$.

> **Intuition.** K-means is the hard-assignment limit of EM for GMMs — it assigns each point to exactly one cluster, which is appropriate when clusters are well-separated. When clusters overlap or have complex covariance structure, soft assignments (GMM) are strictly better. The hard-to-soft transition is controlled by temperature in annealed EM, or by the noise variance $\sigma^2$ in PPCA.

> **Usage.** BIC penalizes parameter count by $\frac{1}{2}k\log N$, making it consistent (selects the true $K$ asymptotically). AIC penalizes by $k$, favoring more complex models — better for prediction, worse for model identification. The Dirichlet Process GMM (DP-GMM) places a nonparametric prior on $K$, automatically growing the number of components as $N$ increases — implemented in `sklearn.mixture.BayesianGaussianMixture`.

> **Practicality.** For normalizing flows (RealNVP, Glow, neural spline flows), the key property is that the Jacobian of the invertible transformation is tractable to compute, enabling exact log-likelihood evaluation. This makes them strictly superior to GMMs for complex continuous densities. For tabular/low-dimensional data: GMMs remain competitive and much cheaper to fit. For high-dimensional data (images, text): normalizing flows, VAEs, or diffusion models are standard. Degenerate GMM solutions in practice: use `reg_covar=1e-6` in `sklearn.mixture.GaussianMixture` to add $10^{-6}\boldsymbol{I}$ to each $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$ during training.

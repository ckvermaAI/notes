# Matrix Decompositions

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 4*

---

## 4.1 Determinant and Trace

### Determinant

$\det(\boldsymbol{A})$ for $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$: the signed volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the columns. Key properties:
- $\det(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B}) = \det(\boldsymbol{A})\det(\boldsymbol{B})$; $\det(\boldsymbol{A}^T) = \det(\boldsymbol{A})$; $\det(\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}) = 1/\det(\boldsymbol{A})$
- Scaling a row by $\lambda$ scales $\det$ by $\lambda$; swapping rows flips the sign
- $\boldsymbol{A}$ invertible $\Leftrightarrow$ $\det(\boldsymbol{A}) \neq 0$ $\Leftrightarrow$ $\text{rk}(\boldsymbol{A}) = n$
- Similar matrices share the same determinant

Computation via Gaussian elimination: reduce to triangular form, then $\det(\boldsymbol{T}) = \prod_i T_{ii}$.

### Trace

$\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \sum_i a_{ii}$. Properties: linearity, $\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{AB}) = \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{BA})$, cyclic invariance $\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{ABC}) = \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{CAB})$, basis-independence for linear maps.

### Characteristic Polynomial

$p_\boldsymbol{A}(\lambda) = \det(\boldsymbol{A} - \lambda\boldsymbol{I})$. Roots are eigenvalues. The constant term is $\det(\boldsymbol{A})$; the $(n-1)$-th coefficient involves $\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{A})$.

## 4.2 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

$\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} = \lambda\boldsymbol{x}$, $\boldsymbol{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Equivalent conditions: $\det(\boldsymbol{A} - \lambda\boldsymbol{I}) = 0$; $\text{rk}(\boldsymbol{A} - \lambda\boldsymbol{I}) < n$.

- **Algebraic multiplicity**: multiplicity of $\lambda_i$ as a root of $p_\boldsymbol{A}$.
- **Geometric multiplicity**: $\dim(E_{\lambda_i})$ where $E_{\lambda_i} = \ker(\boldsymbol{A} - \lambda_i\boldsymbol{I})$.
- Geometric multiplicity $\leq$ algebraic multiplicity; equality iff the matrix is not defective.

**Theorem 4.12**: Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent.

**Definition 4.13 (Defective)**: $\boldsymbol{A}$ is defective if it has fewer than $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. A defective matrix cannot have $n$ distinct eigenvalues.

**Theorem 4.14**: $\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A}$ is always symmetric positive semidefinite; it is SPD iff $\text{rk}(\boldsymbol{A}) = n$.

**Connections to determinant/trace**:
$$\det(\boldsymbol{A}) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i, \qquad \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$$

**Spectral Theorem (Theorem 4.15)**: A symmetric matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$ has real eigenvalues and admits an ONB of eigenvectors (i.e., is orthogonally diagonalizable).

## 4.3 Cholesky Decomposition

For $\boldsymbol{A}$ SPD: $\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{L}\boldsymbol{L}^T$ where $\boldsymbol{L}$ is lower triangular with positive diagonal entries. Unique. Key uses:
- Efficient solution of $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{b}$ (two triangular solves, $O(n^2)$ vs $O(n^3)$ for general LU)
- Sampling from multivariate Gaussians: if $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \boldsymbol{L}\boldsymbol{L}^T$, then $\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{L}\boldsymbol{z}$ for $\boldsymbol{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{I})$

## 4.4 Eigendecomposition (Diagonalization)

$\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{P}\boldsymbol{D}\boldsymbol{P}^{-1}$ where $\boldsymbol{D} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ and columns of $\boldsymbol{P}$ are eigenvectors. Requires $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors (non-defective matrix). Distinct eigenvalues are sufficient but not necessary.

For symmetric $\boldsymbol{A}$, by the spectral theorem: $\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{D}\boldsymbol{Q}^T$ with $\boldsymbol{Q}$ orthogonal. The eigendecomposition is then both orthogonal and uniquely characterized (up to eigenvector sign and degenerate subspaces).

## 4.5 Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

For any $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:
$$\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{V}^T$$
where $\boldsymbol{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ (left singular vectors, eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{A}^T$), $\boldsymbol{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (right singular vectors, eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A}$), $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ diagonal with $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A})} \geq 0$.

Geometric decomposition: $\boldsymbol{V}^T$ (basis change in domain) → $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ (scale/project) → $\boldsymbol{U}$ (basis change in codomain).

**SVD vs Eigendecomposition**:
- SVD applies to all matrices; eigendecomposition requires square, non-defective.
- $\boldsymbol{U}, \boldsymbol{V}$ are orthogonal; $\boldsymbol{P}$ in eigendecomposition may not be.
- $\sigma_i \geq 0$; eigenvalues can be negative or complex.
- $\text{rk}(\boldsymbol{A})$ = number of nonzero singular values.

---

## 4.6 Matrix Approximation via SVD (Eckart-Young)

$$\boldsymbol{A} = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i \boldsymbol{u}_i \boldsymbol{v}_i^T$$

The best rank-$k$ approximation (under both $\|\cdot\|_2$ and Frobenius norm):
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{A}}_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i \boldsymbol{u}_i \boldsymbol{v}_i^T, \qquad \|\boldsymbol{A} - \hat{\boldsymbol{A}}_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$$

This is the mathematical basis for PCA (Chapter 10), image compression, and recommendation systems. The compression ratio is $(m + n + 1) \times k$ vs $m \times n$ entries.

## 4.7 Matrix Taxonomy

| Class | Properties | Decomposition |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| All $\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ | — | SVD |
| Square | Determinant, trace defined | — |
| Invertible | $\det \neq 0$, full rank | $LU$, LU with pivoting |
| Non-defective | $n$ indep. eigenvectors | $PDP^{-1}$ |
| Normal ($\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{A}^T$) | — | Orthogonal diagonalization |
| Orthogonal | $\boldsymbol{A}^T = \boldsymbol{A}^{-1}$ | Product of Givens rotations |
| Symmetric | Real eigenvalues | $\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{D}\boldsymbol{Q}^T$ |
| SPD | All positive eigenvalues | Cholesky $\boldsymbol{L}\boldsymbol{L}^T$, then eigendecomp |

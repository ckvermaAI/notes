## 12.1 Separating Hyperplanes

Binary classification: predict $y \in \{+1, -1\}$ from $\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D$.

A hyperplane is $\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle + b = 0$. The decision function:
$$f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle + b$$

classifies as $+1$ if $f(\boldsymbol{x}) \geq 0$ and $-1$ otherwise. The normal vector $\boldsymbol{w}$ is orthogonal to the hyperplane: for any $\boldsymbol{x}_a, \boldsymbol{x}_b$ on the plane, $\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x}_a - \boldsymbol{x}_b \rangle = 0$.

Training constraint: $y_n(\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x}_n \rangle + b) \geq 0$ for all $n$.

> **Intuition.** A separating hyperplane divides $\mathbb{R}^D$ into two half-spaces. If the data is linearly separable, infinitely many hyperplanes correctly classify all training points. The SVM question is: which one should we pick? The answer (§12.2) is: the one with the largest margin — the one that is furthest from both classes. $\boldsymbol{w}$ determines orientation, $b$ determines offset along $\boldsymbol{w}$.

> **Usage.** The hyperplane classifier $f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle + b$ is the fundamental linear model: it is equivalent to logistic regression at the decision boundary (both produce linear classifiers in feature space). The SVM and logistic regression differ in how they place the boundary: SVM maximizes margin (discriminative, geometric), logistic regression maximizes likelihood (probabilistic). In the linearly separable case, SVM is uniquely defined; logistic regression is not (likelihood does not saturate until $\|\boldsymbol{w}\| \to \infty$).

> **Practicality.** The signed distance of a point $\boldsymbol{x}$ from the hyperplane is $f(\boldsymbol{x}) / \|\boldsymbol{w}\|$. The sign gives the class; the magnitude gives the confidence. In the canonical SVM (§12.2), the hyperplane is scaled so that the support hyperplanes are at $f = \pm 1$, making the margin exactly $2/\|\boldsymbol{w}\|$.

## 12.2 Primal SVM

### Margin

Among all separating hyperplanes, SVMs select the one maximizing the **margin** $= 2/\|\boldsymbol{w}\|$, which is the distance between the support hyperplanes $\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle + b = \pm 1$.

By the rescaling argument (Theorem 12.1), maximizing margin is equivalent to the **hard margin SVM**:
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{w}, b} \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_n(\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x}_n \rangle + b) \geq 1 \;\forall n$$

Maximizing margin improves generalization: larger margin $\Leftrightarrow$ lower Vapnik-Chervonenkis dimension $\Leftrightarrow$ tighter generalization bound.

> **Intuition.** Maximum margin is motivated by two complementary arguments. (1) Geometric: a large margin means the classifier is robust to small perturbations of training points — you have more room before a point crosses the boundary. (2) Theoretical: for linear classifiers with margin $\gamma$ on data in a ball of radius $R$, the VC generalization bound scales as $O(R^2/\gamma^2 N)$, independent of the input dimension $D$. Larger margin directly tightens the bound, explaining why SVMs often generalize well even in very high-dimensional spaces.

> **Usage.** The hard margin SVM requires linear separability. In practice, use the soft margin SVM (below). The hard margin form is conceptually cleaner for understanding the geometry and for deriving the dual. The rescaling argument — that we can always normalize the hyperplane so that the minimum functional margin is 1 — is what converts the non-convex "maximize $1/\|\boldsymbol{w}\|$" into the convex "minimize $\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2$".

> **Practicality.** The hard margin SVM is only applicable to perfectly linearly separable data. In practice, even if data is separable, the hard margin SVM may overfit if a few outliers happen to be close to the margin — the soft margin SVM is almost always preferred.

### Soft Margin SVM

For non-linearly separable data, introduce slack variables $\xi_n \geq 0$:
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{w}, b, \boldsymbol{\xi}} \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 + C\sum_n \xi_n \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_n(\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x}_n \rangle + b) \geq 1 - \xi_n, \; \xi_n \geq 0$$

$C > 0$ trades off margin size vs. training error. Large $C$ → hard margin (low bias, high variance); small $C$ → large margin (high bias, low variance). Note $\boldsymbol{w}$ is regularized; $b$ is not.

> **Intuition.** Slack variables $\xi_n$ allow misclassification: a point can be inside or even on the wrong side of the margin, with cost $\xi_n$ (the amount by which the margin constraint is violated). The term $C\sum_n \xi_n$ penalizes these violations. $C$ is the regularization parameter: small $C$ → willing to tolerate many margin violations → wider margin → more regularized → lower variance, higher bias. This is the classic bias-variance tradeoff controlled by a single scalar.

> **Usage.** The soft margin SVM is the standard SVM for real data. $C$ is tuned via cross-validation, typically on a log scale (e.g., $C \in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-2}, \ldots, 10^3\}$). In sklearn's `SVC`, $C$ plays this role directly.

> **Practicality.** $b$ is not regularized — this is a deliberate design choice. Regularizing $b$ would bias predictions toward $y = 0$ when $\boldsymbol{x}$ is near the origin, which is undesirable since the origin is an arbitrary location in feature space. In contrast, not regularizing $b$ ensures that the classifier can shift its decision boundary to wherever the data supports, independent of the coordinate origin.

### Hinge Loss View

The soft margin SVM is equivalent to ERM with the **hinge loss** $\ell(t) = \max(0, 1-t)$:
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{w}, b} \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 + C\sum_n \max(0, 1 - y_n(\langle \boldsymbol{w}, \boldsymbol{x}_n \rangle + b))$$

The $\frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2$ term is the $L_2$ regularizer (equivalent to Gaussian prior from the MAP perspective).

> **Intuition.** The hinge loss $\max(0, 1-t)$ is zero when the example is correctly classified with margin $\geq 1$ (no penalty for "confident" correct predictions) and grows linearly for wrong or low-confidence predictions. Compared to the logistic loss ($\log(1 + e^{-t})$), the hinge loss is exactly zero for correctly classified examples — it is "sparse" in the dual. This is what makes SVMs sparse: only the support vectors (hinge loss > 0) contribute to the solution.

> **Usage.** The hinge loss view unifies SVMs with the ERM framework (§8.2), making it clear that SVMs are just another regularized empirical risk minimizer — the difference from logistic regression is the choice of loss. This also enables subgradient descent as a direct solver for $\boldsymbol{w}$ without forming the dual, which is useful for large $N$ where the dual QP is expensive.

> **Practicality.** `sklearn.svm.LinearSVC` uses the hinge loss + L2 regularization directly, implementing a primal solver (LibLinear) — faster than the dual QP solver for large $N$. `sklearn.linear_model.SGDClassifier(loss='hinge')` uses subgradient SGD for hinge loss — useful for streaming or very large datasets where even LinearSVC is slow.

## 12.3 Dual SVM

The Lagrangian of the primal soft margin SVM:
$$\mathfrak{L}(\boldsymbol{w}, b, \boldsymbol{\xi}, \boldsymbol{\alpha}, \boldsymbol{\gamma}) = \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 + C\sum_n\xi_n - \sum_n\alpha_n(y_n(\langle\boldsymbol{w},\boldsymbol{x}_n\rangle + b) - 1 + \xi_n) - \sum_n\gamma_n\xi_n$$

KKT stationarity conditions:
- $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{w}}\mathfrak{L} = 0 \Rightarrow \boldsymbol{w} = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n \boldsymbol{x}_n$ (**representer theorem**)
- $\nabla_b\mathfrak{L} = 0 \Rightarrow \sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0$
- $\nabla_{\xi_n}\mathfrak{L} = 0 \Rightarrow \alpha_n = C - \gamma_n \leq C$ (since $\gamma_n \geq 0$)

Substituting back gives the **dual QP**:
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} y_i y_j \alpha_i \alpha_j \langle \boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j \rangle - \sum_i \alpha_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_n y_n \alpha_n = 0,\; 0 \leq \alpha_n \leq C$$

**Support vectors**: examples with $\alpha_n > 0$. From complementary slackness:
- $0 < \alpha_n < C$: $\xi_n = 0$, $\boldsymbol{x}_n$ on the margin boundary
- $\alpha_n = C$: $\xi_n > 0$, $\boldsymbol{x}_n$ inside or beyond the margin

Bias $b^*$ recovered from any support vector on the margin: $b^* = y_n - \langle \boldsymbol{w}^*, \boldsymbol{x}_n \rangle$.

**Why dual?** The dual scales with $N$ (not $D$), advantageous when $D \gg N$. More importantly, it exposes the inner product structure enabling kernelization.

> **Intuition.** The representer theorem $\boldsymbol{w}^* = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n \boldsymbol{x}_n$ is the key: the optimal weight vector is a linear combination of training examples (with signed dual variables as coefficients). Only examples with $\alpha_n > 0$ — the support vectors — contribute to $\boldsymbol{w}^*$. This sparsity is a consequence of complementary slackness: non-support vectors satisfy their margin constraints strictly, so their Lagrange multipliers are zero. Geometrically, support vectors are the examples that sit on or inside the margin, defining where the margin boundaries are.

> **Usage.** The dual is the standard formulation for kernel SVMs. Since $\boldsymbol{w}$ appears only through inner products $\langle \boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j \rangle$ in the dual objective and $\langle \boldsymbol{w}^*, \boldsymbol{x}_* \rangle = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n \langle \boldsymbol{x}_n, \boldsymbol{x}_* \rangle$ in prediction, every inner product can be replaced by a kernel $k(\boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j)$. This is the kernel trick (§12.4). The dual is also used to compute the duality gap as a convergence certificate.

> **Practicality.** Support vectors are typically 10–50% of the training set for well-tuned SVMs. Fewer support vectors = faster prediction (prediction time scales with number of support vectors, not $N$). If nearly all training points are support vectors, $C$ is too large (underfitting the margin, the model has essentially memorized the data). The constraint $0 \leq \alpha_n \leq C$ (box constraint) is what distinguishes the soft margin dual from the hard margin dual ($0 \leq \alpha_n$).

### Geometric (Convex Hull) View

The hard margin SVM finds the minimum-norm vector $\boldsymbol{w} = \boldsymbol{c} - \boldsymbol{d}$ where $\boldsymbol{c} \in \text{conv}(\{\boldsymbol{x}_n : y_n = +1\})$ and $\boldsymbol{d} \in \text{conv}(\{\boldsymbol{x}_n : y_n = -1\})$ are the closest points of the respective convex hulls.

> **Intuition.** The maximum margin hyperplane bisects the shortest line segment connecting the convex hulls of the two classes. This is the most geometrically natural separator: it is equidistant from both classes at the closest pair of points. The support vectors are the training examples that define (lie on) those closest convex hull points. When classes overlap (soft margin), this picture extends to reduced convex hulls with $\alpha_n \leq C$ bounding the expansion.

> **Practicality.** This convex hull view is not just aesthetic — it makes the well-posedness of the SVM obvious: there is a unique point in each convex hull that is closest to the other, hence a unique $\boldsymbol{w}$ (up to sign conventions). It also explains why SVMs are robust to class-irrelevant features: rotating the data by adding noisy dimensions does not change the relative position of the class convex hulls.

## 12.4 Kernels

The dual depends on data only through inner products $\langle \boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j \rangle$. The **kernel trick** replaces these with $k(\boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j) = \langle \phi(\boldsymbol{x}_i), \phi(\boldsymbol{x}_j) \rangle_\mathcal{H}$ for some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature map $\phi$.

**Valid kernel** $\Leftrightarrow$ the $N \times N$ Gram matrix $\boldsymbol{K}_{ij} = k(\boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j)$ is symmetric positive semidefinite for any input set.

Common kernels:
- **Linear**: $k(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}') = \langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}' \rangle$
- **Polynomial**: $k(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}') = (\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}' \rangle + c)^d$ — implicitly maps to all monomials up to degree $d$
- **RBF (Gaussian)**: $k(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}') = \exp(-\|\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{x}'\|^2 / 2\ell^2)$ — corresponds to infinite-dimensional RKHS

The prediction for a new point uses $f(\boldsymbol{x}_*) = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n k(\boldsymbol{x}_n, \boldsymbol{x}_*) + b$, evaluating the kernel only at support vectors.

> **Intuition.** The kernel trick allows SVMs to classify nonlinearly in the original space while remaining linear classifiers in a (potentially infinite-dimensional) feature space. Computing $k(\boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j)$ directly is $O(D)$ even when the implicit feature map $\phi$ has dimension $D^d$ or $\infty$ — the inner product in feature space is free. The RKHS (Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space) is the Hilbert space of functions induced by the kernel; every function in the RKHS can be written as $f(\cdot) = \sum_n \alpha_n k(\boldsymbol{x}_n, \cdot)$ — this is the representer theorem in the kernel setting.

> **Usage.** RBF kernel is the default kernel when you don't know the structure of the data — it has universal approximation properties (it can approximate any continuous function on a compact set). Polynomial kernels are useful for text/structured data. String kernels, graph kernels, tree kernels extend the SVM to non-vectorial inputs. The kernel perspective is foundational to understanding attention mechanisms (softmax attention ≈ kernel regression with exponential kernel), neural tangent kernels (infinite-width NNs behave like kernel machines), and Gaussian process inference.

> **Practicality.** The RBF kernel has a bandwidth hyperparameter $\ell$ (or equivalently, `gamma = 1/(2\ell^2)` in sklearn). Both $C$ and $\gamma$ must be tuned jointly — use grid search or random search on a log scale. The Gram matrix $\boldsymbol{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ requires $O(N^2 D)$ to compute and $O(N^2)$ memory — this limits exact kernel SVMs to $N \lesssim 10^5$. For larger datasets, use the Nyström approximation (random subset of training points to approximate $\boldsymbol{K}$) or random Fourier features (approximate the RBF kernel with an explicit finite-dimensional feature map).

## 12.5 Numerical Solution

**Unconstrained** (hinge loss + gradient descent): subgradient $\partial\ell(t) = -1$ if $t < 1$, $0$ if $t > 1$, any value in $[-1, 0]$ at $t = 1$.

**QP solvers**: primal (direct, $O(N^3)$ in general) or dual (coordinate descent on $\alpha$, e.g., SMO algorithm which decomposes into 2-variable subproblems). LIBSVM implements SMO with caching; scales to millions of examples.

> **Intuition.** The hinge loss is piecewise linear — not differentiable at $t = 1$, but the subgradient (any value in the subdifferential) suffices for subgradient descent, which converges at $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ rate (slower than gradient descent's $O(1/T)$ for smooth functions). The SMO insight is that 2-variable QP subproblems have an exact analytical solution (it's just a constrained quadratic in one variable after fixing the other), so you never need an external QP solver.

> **Usage.** Primal solvers (LibLinear, SGDClassifier) are faster for linear SVMs with large $N$. Dual solvers (LibSVM via `sklearn.svm.SVC`) are faster for kernel SVMs and when $N$ is moderate. For $N > 10^5$ with non-linear kernels, approximate methods (Nyström + LinearSVC, or deep networks) are standard.

> **Practicality.** `sklearn.svm.SVC` wraps LibSVM (dual, SMO) — default for kernel SVMs. `sklearn.svm.LinearSVC` wraps LibLinear (primal, coordinate descent) — faster for linear SVMs by 10–100x. `sklearn.linear_model.SGDClassifier(loss='hinge', penalty='l2')` is the online/streaming variant. When comparing SVMs to neural networks, always use LibSVM/LibLinear as the baseline — many published comparisons underperform SVMs because they use suboptimal hyperparameters.

## 12.6 Further Reading

- **Theoretical guarantees**: margin-based generalization bounds (Vapnik-Chervonenkis theory); PAC learning. SVMs have one of the tightest classical generalization bounds.
- **Probabilistic outputs**: Platt scaling calibrates SVM scores to probabilities via sigmoid fit on held-out data.
- **Multi-class**: one-vs-one or one-vs-rest decomposition; Crammer-Singer formulation gives a joint multi-class objective.
- **Gaussian process classification**: Bayesian analogue using a GP prior on $f$; posterior is non-Gaussian (requires Laplace approximation or EP).
- **Modern relevance**: SVMs are largely superseded by deep networks for perception tasks but remain competitive on tabular/structured data and in low-data regimes. The kernel perspective is foundational for understanding attention (dot-product attention ≈ kernel regression) and neural tangent kernels.

> **Intuition.** VC theory gives dimension-free generalization bounds for SVMs: the error on unseen data is at most $O(\sqrt{(R/\gamma)^2 / N + \log(1/\delta)/N})$ with probability $1-\delta$, depending only on the ratio $R/\gamma$ (data radius divided by margin), not on $D$. This is why SVMs work in very high-dimensional spaces — as long as the margin is large, the dimension is irrelevant.

> **Usage.** Platt scaling is a standard post-hoc calibration: fit a sigmoid $p(y=1) = \sigma(Af(\boldsymbol{x}) + B)$ to the SVM scores on a held-out calibration set. This is necessary when SVM scores are used as probabilities (e.g., in downstream Bayesian models or for reliable confidence estimates). For multi-class, one-vs-one (trains $K(K-1)/2$ classifiers) is generally more accurate than one-vs-rest but slower at prediction time.

> **Practicality.** When to use SVMs in 2026: (1) tabular data with $N < 10^5$ and no obvious deep learning advantage, (2) small-data regimes where gradient boosting and SVMs are competitive, (3) when you need a theoretically well-understood model with reliable generalization bounds, (4) when computing kernel evaluations between structured objects (strings, graphs, sequences) where explicit features are hard to define. The neural tangent kernel connection means understanding SVMs + kernels directly informs understanding of infinite-width neural networks in the lazy training regime.

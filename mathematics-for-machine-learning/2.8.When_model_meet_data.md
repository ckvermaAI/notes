# When Models Meet Data

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 8*

---

## 8.1 Data, Models, and Learning

**Data**: $N$ examples as feature vectors $\boldsymbol{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^D$, labels $y_n$. Assumes preprocessing to numerical form, normalization (zero mean, unit variance per feature).

**Models**: either as functions $f(\boldsymbol{x}; \boldsymbol{\theta})$ (ERM view) or as probability distributions $p(y \mid \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ (probabilistic view).

**Learning**: three phases:
1. *Parameter estimation* on training data
2. *Hyperparameter tuning / model selection*
3. *Inference / prediction* on new inputs

> **Intuition.** The functional view (minimize a loss) and the probabilistic view (maximize a likelihood) are two languages for the same thing. The probabilistic view is strictly more expressive: it can represent uncertainty in predictions, handle missing data naturally, and compose with Bayesian inference. The functional view is computationally simpler and often sufficient when calibrated uncertainty is not needed.

> **Usage.** The three-phase decomposition is universal. Phase 1 uses training data with a fixed objective; phase 2 uses held-out validation data (or cross-validation) to select the objective/hypothesis class itself; phase 3 is inference. Conflating these phases — e.g., tuning hyperparameters on test data — is the most common source of optimistically biased evaluation in ML practice.

> **Practicality.** Always establish a clean train/val/test split before modeling. For small datasets, nested cross-validation (§8.6) separates hyperparameter selection from generalization estimation. Normalization is not just numerical hygiene: it conditions the optimization problem, making gradient descent converge faster and making regularization scale-invariant across features.

## 8.2 Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM)

**Empirical risk**: $R_\text{emp}(f) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \ell(y_n, f(\boldsymbol{x}_n))$ — approximates the true risk $R_\text{true}(f) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y}[\ell(y, f(x))]$ (unknown, evaluated via test set).

**Regularization** prevents overfitting by penalizing model complexity:
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \frac{1}{N}\|\boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 + \lambda\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$$

**$K$-fold cross-validation**: split data into $K$ folds, train on $K-1$, validate on 1, cycle. Estimates generalization error without touching the test set. More reliable than a single train/val split but $K\times$ more expensive.

> **Intuition.** ERM is the workhorse: if you can write down a loss, you can minimize it. The catch is that minimizing the empirical risk is not the same as minimizing the true risk — the gap is the **generalization error**. With enough capacity, any model can interpolate the training data (zero empirical risk) while failing completely on unseen data. Regularization explicitly trades training performance for generalization by keeping model complexity bounded.

> **Usage.** The empirical risk is what every practical optimizer minimizes. The choice of $\ell$ encodes your noise model: squared error assumes Gaussian noise; cross-entropy assumes Bernoulli/categorical; hinge loss makes no distributional assumption but has good margin properties. Regularization strength $\lambda$ is almost always a hyperparameter tuned via cross-validation.

> **Practicality.** $K=5$ or $K=10$ folds is standard. For $K = N$ (leave-one-out), variance of the estimate drops but cost becomes $O(N)$ training runs. Stratified $K$-fold maintains class proportions in each fold and is important for imbalanced datasets. For time-series, use temporal splits (train on past, validate on future) rather than random splits to avoid look-ahead bias.

## 8.3 Parameter Estimation via Maximum Likelihood

**Negative log-likelihood** (NLL): $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\sum_n \log p(y_n \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ (i.i.d. assumption decomposes the joint into a product, which log converts to a sum).

**Gaussian noise model**: $p(y_n \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(y_n \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n^T\boldsymbol{\theta}, \sigma^2)$ → NLL $\propto \|\boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$ — MLE and least-squares are equivalent under Gaussian noise.

**MAP estimation** (priors as regularizers): add $-\log p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ to NLL. Gaussian prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2\boldsymbol{I})$ yields $L_2$ regularization with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$. Laplace prior yields $L_1$ (LASSO).

**Overfitting in MLE**: MLE minimizes training loss but can overfit with small $N$ or complex models. MAP/regularization and early stopping are the standard mitigations; full Bayesian treatment addresses it by integrating over parameters.

> **Intuition.** The i.i.d. assumption turns the likelihood of the entire dataset into a product — and taking the log converts the product into a sum, making the gradient of the NLL simply the sum of per-sample gradients. This is why SGD works: each mini-batch is an unbiased estimate of the full gradient of the NLL. The equivalence of MLE to least-squares under Gaussian noise is not a coincidence: the Gaussian is the maximum entropy distribution given fixed variance, and least-squares is the minimum-variance unbiased estimator (Gauss-Markov theorem).

> **Usage.** MLE is the default approach for fitting parametric models. The noise model choice is a modeling decision: Gaussian → least squares; Bernoulli → logistic regression (binary cross-entropy); Poisson → log-linear model (count data); Student-t → robust regression (heavy-tailed residuals). Each corresponds to a different NLL and a different loss function.

> **Practicality.** In deep learning, NLL with the appropriate likelihood *is* the training loss. Cross-entropy for classification, mean squared error for regression — these are not arbitrary choices, they are the NLL under specific noise models. MAP with Gaussian prior *is* L2 regularization (weight decay). Knowing these correspondences lets you interpret hyperparameters like weight decay as prior precision $1/\tau^2$, guiding reasonable initialization ranges.

## 8.4 Probabilistic Modeling and Inference

### Bayesian Inference

$$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{X}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{X} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{X})}, \quad p(\mathcal{X}) = \int p(\mathcal{X} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

Prediction marginalizes over the posterior: $p(\boldsymbol{x}) = \int p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{X})\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — this is the Bayesian predictive distribution. It naturally encodes model uncertainty.

Intractable for most non-conjugate models; approximated by MCMC (exact samples asymptotically) or variational inference (tractable lower bound).

> **Intuition.** Bayesian inference is about updating a distribution over hypotheses (parameters) with evidence (data). The posterior is not a single best-fit model but a probability distribution over all models weighted by how well they explain the data and how plausible they were a priori. The predictive distribution integrates out this uncertainty — so predictions come with calibrated confidence intervals rather than point estimates. This is the only statistically coherent way to handle uncertainty about parameters.

> **Usage.** Bayesian linear regression (§9.3) is the cleanest closed-form example. Beyond that, Bayesian NNs model weight uncertainty; Gaussian processes are Bayesian regression over function spaces; Bayesian optimization uses a GP posterior to guide black-box function optimization (hyperparameter search, experimental design).

> **Practicality.** The marginal likelihood $p(\mathcal{X})$ is intractable except in conjugate models (Gaussian likelihood + Gaussian prior). MCMC (HMC, NUTS via Stan/PyMC) is the gold standard for moderate-dimensional posteriors; variational inference (mean-field, normalizing flows) trades exactness for speed and is standard in deep generative models (VAEs). For large-scale models, Laplace approximation (Gaussian fit to the posterior at the MAP) is a lightweight way to get approximate posteriors from any trained neural network.

### Latent Variable Models

Introduce latent $\boldsymbol{z}$: $p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \int p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{z}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{z})\,d\boldsymbol{z}$. This marginalization is typically intractable — handled by EM (coordinate ascent on the ELBO) or variational inference. Examples: GMMs (Chapter 11), PPCA/FA, VAEs.

> **Intuition.** Latent variables are the "hidden causes" of observed data. The model says: data is generated by first sampling a latent state (cluster assignment, low-dimensional code, topic) and then sampling the observation conditioned on that state. Marginalizing over the latent variable gives the observed data distribution, but inference about the latent state given observed data ($p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x})$) is the posterior — and this is typically what you want (e.g., cluster assignment probabilities, latent codes).

> **Usage.** GMMs for density estimation and soft clustering (§11); PPCA/FA for linear dimensionality reduction with noise model (§10.7); VAE for nonlinear latent representations with generative model; HMMs for sequences; topic models (LDA) for text. The latent variable structure is also the basis of all deep generative models (flow models, diffusion models, GANs can be understood as implicitly defining a latent variable model).

> **Practicality.** The EM algorithm is the workhorse for latent variable models with tractable E-step posteriors. For intractable posteriors (most deep models), the ELBO is maximized with respect to both the model parameters and a variational distribution $q(\boldsymbol{z})$. The reparameterization trick (sampling $\boldsymbol{z} = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$) enables backprop through the sampling step, making VAE training feasible with standard autodiff.

## 8.5 Directed Graphical Models

**Joint factorization**: $p(\boldsymbol{x}) = \prod_k p(x_k \mid \text{Pa}_k)$, where $\text{Pa}_k$ are the parents of node $k$ in the DAG.

**d-separation**: a graph-theoretic criterion for conditional independence. $\mathcal{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathcal{B} \mid \mathcal{C}$ iff every path from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$ is blocked by $\mathcal{C}$ (blocked = passes through a non-collider in $\mathcal{C}$, or a collider not in $\mathcal{C}$ and with no descendant in $\mathcal{C}$).

> **Intuition.** A directed graphical model (Bayesian network) is a compact notation for a joint distribution: it encodes which variables are conditionally independent given others, and specifies the joint as a product of conditional distributions. The DAG structure is a causal hypothesis — arrows represent direct dependencies. d-separation is the algorithm that reads off all implied conditional independencies from the graph structure without computing the full joint.

> **Usage.** Bayesian networks are the standard formalism for probabilistic reasoning under uncertainty: medical diagnosis (disease → symptoms), causal inference, hidden Markov models (linear chain), Kalman filters, naive Bayes classifier (class → features, all conditionally independent given class). Plate notation compresses i.i.d. repeated structure (observations $\boldsymbol{x}_n$ conditional on shared parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$).

> **Practicality.** In ML, graphical models are most useful as a modeling language rather than as inference machines. Drawing the generative model clarifies the factorization structure, identifies which parameters to share, and makes the EM algorithm straightforward to derive. The three canonical structures to internalize: **chain** (Markov property, HMM), **fork** ($A \leftarrow C \rightarrow B$, $C$ blocks $A$-$B$ dependence when observed), and **collider** ($A \rightarrow C \leftarrow B$, conditioning on $C$ *induces* $A$-$B$ dependence — the explaining-away effect).

## 8.6 Model Selection

### Nested Cross-Validation
- Inner loop: hyperparameter selection (validation set)
- Outer loop: generalization estimation (test set)

Prevents the "peeking" problem where test performance leaks into hyperparameter tuning.

> **Intuition.** Without nesting, you are implicitly using the test set to select hyperparameters, optimistically biasing the generalization estimate. Nested CV keeps the outer test folds completely unseen during the entire model selection process — the outer loop gives an unbiased estimate of generalization error of the *model selection procedure itself*, not just of a fixed model.

> **Practicality.** Nested CV is expensive ($K_\text{outer} \times K_\text{inner}$ training runs). For large datasets, a fixed outer hold-out set (test split) combined with $K$-fold inner CV is standard. For final deployment, retrain on all available data with the best-selected hyperparameters. The outer test set is single-use: once evaluated, it must not be used to guide further decisions.

### Bayesian Model Selection

$$p(\mathcal{M}_k \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mathcal{M}_k)\,p(\mathcal{M}_k)$$

The **marginal likelihood** $p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mathcal{M}_k) = \int p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_k, \mathcal{M}_k)\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k \mid \mathcal{M}_k)\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$ automatically implements Occam's razor: complex models spread probability over larger parameter spaces, reducing the marginal likelihood unless the data justifies the complexity. This is the Bayesian alternative to cross-validation.

**Bayes factor**: $\frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mathcal{M}_1)}{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mathcal{M}_2)}$ — ratio of marginal likelihoods, independent of priors on models.

> **Intuition.** The marginal likelihood integrates out parameters, penalizing models that spread probability mass across a large parameter space. A simple model that fits the data well beats a complex model that fits marginally better by concentrating its probability on a small region of parameter space — this is the Bayesian Occam's razor. It is automatic: you do not need to add an explicit complexity penalty (like BIC/AIC), the prior does the work.

> **Usage.** Evidence maximization (type-II MLE) maximizes the marginal likelihood over *hyperparameters* (not parameters), treating the prior as a hyperparameter. In Gaussian processes and Bayesian linear regression, the marginal likelihood is analytically tractable and is the standard method for selecting kernel hyperparameters, regularization strength, and feature dimension.

> **Practicality.** Computing the marginal likelihood requires integrating over the full parameter space — tractable for Gaussian/conjugate models, intractable otherwise. Approximations: Laplace approximation (Gaussian fit at the MAP, gives BIC asymptotically), variational lower bound (ELBO), or AIC/BIC as fast non-Bayesian proxies. The Bayes factor scale: $\ln(\text{BF}) > 3$ is considered strong evidence for model 1 over model 2.

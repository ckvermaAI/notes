# Probability and Distributions

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 6*

## 6.1 Probability Space

A probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{A}, P)$: sample space $\Omega$, $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}$ (event space), probability measure $P: \mathcal{A} \to [0,1]$ with $P(\Omega) = 1$.

A **random variable** $X: \Omega \to T$ is a measurable function mapping outcomes to a target space $T$. It is neither random nor a variable — it is a deterministic function encoding which property of the outcome we care about.

**Probability vs statistics**: probability derives outcomes from a known model; statistics infers the model from observed outcomes. ML is statistics.

---

## 6.2 Discrete and Continuous Probabilities

**Discrete**: $P(X = x)$ via the pmf. Joint, marginal, conditional follow the usual rules.

**Continuous**: $p(x) \geq 0$ with $\int p(x)\,dx = 1$ (pdf). Note $p(x)$ can exceed 1 (it's a density, not a probability). The cdf: $F_X(\boldsymbol{x}) = P(X_1 \leq x_1, \ldots, X_D \leq x_D)$.

**Multivariate Gaussian**:
$$p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{D/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

## 6.3 Sum Rule, Product Rule, Bayes' Theorem

$$p(\boldsymbol{x}) = \int p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y})\,d\boldsymbol{y} \quad \text{(marginalization)}$$
$$p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}) = p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})\,p(\boldsymbol{x}) \quad \text{(product rule)}$$

**Bayes' theorem**:
$$p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{y}) = \frac{p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})\,p(\boldsymbol{x})}{p(\boldsymbol{y})}, \quad p(\boldsymbol{y}) = \int p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})\,p(\boldsymbol{x})\,d\boldsymbol{x}$$

- **Prior** $p(\boldsymbol{x})$: belief before observing data; must be nonzero for all plausible $\boldsymbol{x}$.
- **Likelihood** $p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{x})$: a function of $\boldsymbol{x}$ (not a distribution over $\boldsymbol{y}$).
- **Posterior** $p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{y})$: updated belief; the object of interest in Bayesian inference.
- **Evidence** $p(\boldsymbol{y})$: normalizing constant; intractable in general (requires marginalizing over $\boldsymbol{x}$). Also used for Bayesian model selection.

The marginalization integral is computationally hard in high dimensions (no polynomial-time exact algorithm in general) — motivating variational inference, MCMC, and other approximations.

## 6.4 Summary Statistics and Independence

**Expected value**: $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g(x)p(x)\,dx$ (continuous) or $\sum_x g(x)p(x)$ (discrete).

**Covariance** (univariate): $\text{Cov}[x,y] = \mathbb{E}[xy] - \mathbb{E}[x]\mathbb{E}[y]$.

**Covariance** (multivariate):
$$\text{Cov}[\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}] = \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{x}\boldsymbol{y}^T] - \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{x}]\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{y}]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times E}$$

**Independence**: $p(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}) = p(\boldsymbol{x})p(\boldsymbol{y})$. Independence implies $\text{Cov} = 0$; the converse is false in general (only true for Gaussians).

## 6.5 Common Distributions

### Bernoulli

Models a single binary outcome — coin flip, click/no-click, positive/negative label.

Parameter: $\mu \in [0,1]$ — probability of success.

$P(x \mid \mu) = \mu^x (1-\mu)^{1-x}$, where $x \in \{0, 1\}$.

Mean: $\mu$. Variance: $\mu(1-\mu)$.

ML use: binary classification output layer with sigmoid activation. Logistic regression is exactly MLE under a Bernoulli likelihood.

### Binomial

Models the number of successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli trials with the same probability $\mu$.

$P(k \mid n, \mu) = \binom{n}{k} \mu^k (1-\mu)^{n-k}$, where $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$.

Mean: $n\mu$. Variance: $n\mu(1-\mu)$.

Bernoulli is the special case $n=1$. ML use: modeling counts, A/B testing, aggregated binary outcomes.

### Multinomial

Generalizes Binomial to more than two outcomes — rolling a K-sided die $n$ times.

Parameters: $n$ trials, probability vector $\boldsymbol{\mu} = [\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_K]$ with $\sum_k \mu_k = 1$.

$P(x_1, \ldots, x_K \mid n, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \frac{n!}{x_1! \cdots x_K!} \prod_{k=1}^K \mu_k^{x_k}$, where $\sum_k x_k = n$.

For $n=1$ this is the **Categorical distribution** — single draw from K classes. ML use: softmax output layer in multiclass classification, language model next-token prediction, topic models.

### Conjugacy (plain terms)

When you do Bayesian inference, you start with a prior on parameters, observe data, and compute a posterior. In general this requires a hard integral. Conjugacy is when the prior and posterior belong to the **same family** — the data just updates the numbers (hyperparameters), not the shape of the distribution.

Concretely:

- You believe a coin's bias $\mu$ follows Beta($\alpha$, $\beta$) — your prior.
- You flip the coin and see $h$ heads and $t$ tails.
- The posterior is Beta($\alpha + h$, $\beta + t$) — same family, updated counts.

No integral needed. The Beta prior is conjugate to the Bernoulli/Binomial likelihood.

Similarly, the Dirichlet is conjugate to the Multinomial — which is why Dirichlet priors appear everywhere in topic models (LDA) and language modeling.

**Why it matters in practice**: conjugate models give closed-form Bayesian updates, making them efficient and interpretable. When conjugacy breaks (logistic regression, neural networks), you fall back to approximate inference — MCMC or variational methods.

| Likelihood | Conjugate Prior | Posterior |
|---|---|---|
| Bernoulli / Binomial | Beta($\alpha$, $\beta$) | Beta($\alpha + h$, $\beta + t$) |
| Multinomial / Categorical | Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$) | Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha} + \boldsymbol{c}$) |
| Gaussian (known variance) | Gaussian | Gaussian |
| Gaussian (known mean) | Gamma (on precision) | Gamma |

**Product of Gaussians**: $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{A}) \cdot \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{b}, \boldsymbol{B}) \propto \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{c}, \boldsymbol{C})$ (unnormalized). This is the basis for Kalman filtering and Gaussian process inference.

## 6.6 Conjugacy and the Exponential Family

### Exponential Family

$$p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = h(\boldsymbol{x})\exp\!\big(\langle\boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})\rangle - A(\boldsymbol{\theta})\big)$$

- $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})$: **sufficient statistics** — capture all information about $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ from data.
- $\boldsymbol{\theta}$: **natural parameters**.
- $A(\boldsymbol{\theta})$: **log-partition function** (log-normalizer); its derivatives yield cumulants: $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} A = \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})]$, $\nabla^2_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} A = \text{Cov}[\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})]$.

Unifies Gaussian, Bernoulli, Binomial, Beta, Dirichlet, Poisson, Gamma, etc.

**Advantages**: finite-dimensional sufficient statistics; MLE has closed-form via moment matching ($\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})] = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x}_n)$); concave log-likelihood; systematic conjugate priors.

**Gaussian as exponential family**: $\boldsymbol{\phi}(x) = [x, x^2]^T$, $\boldsymbol{\theta} = [\mu/\sigma^2, -1/(2\sigma^2)]^T$.

**Bernoulli as exponential family**: natural parameter $\theta = \log\frac{\mu}{1-\mu}$ (log-odds), with $\mu = \sigma(\theta)$ (sigmoid). This is the connection to logistic regression.

### Fisher-Neyman Sufficient Statistics

$\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x})$ is sufficient for $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ iff $p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = h(\boldsymbol{x})\,g_\theta(\boldsymbol{\phi}(\boldsymbol{x}))$. Sufficient statistics compress data without information loss about the parameter — foundational in classical statistics and natural gradient methods.

### Conjugate Priors

Every exponential family member has a conjugate prior of the form:
$$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \gamma) \propto \exp\!\big(\langle[\gamma_1, \gamma_2], [\boldsymbol{\theta}, -A(\boldsymbol{\theta})]\rangle\big)$$

Guarantees the posterior stays in the same family, enabling closed-form Bayesian updates. Conjugacy breaks for models combining non-conjugate components (e.g., logistic regression, deep networks) — motivating approximate inference.

# Dimensionality Reduction with PCA

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 10*

---

## 10.1 Problem Setting

Given i.i.d. $\mathcal{X} = \{\boldsymbol{x}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{x}_N\}$, $\boldsymbol{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^D$, mean-centered. Data covariance:
$$\boldsymbol{S} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \boldsymbol{x}_n\boldsymbol{x}_n^T = \frac{1}{N}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{X}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$$

Find $\boldsymbol{B} = [\boldsymbol{b}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{b}_M] \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times M}$ (orthonormal columns) such that low-dimensional codes $\boldsymbol{z}_n = \boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^M$ and reconstructions $\tilde{\boldsymbol{x}}_n = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{z}_n$ are as close as possible to $\boldsymbol{x}_n$.

> **Intuition.** High-dimensional data often lies near a low-dimensional manifold — most of the variation is concentrated in a few directions. PCA finds the linear subspace that captures the most variance, which is the best linear approximation to this manifold. The key assumption is that variance correlates with information: directions of high variance encode structure, directions of low variance encode noise.

> **Usage.** PCA is the universal preprocessing step for high-dimensional data: visualization (project to 2–3 PCs for scatter plots), denoising (discard low-variance components), compression (store low-dimensional codes), decorrelation (PCA outputs are uncorrelated, improving conditioning for downstream learning), speeding up downstream algorithms (fewer features → faster training).

> **Practicality.** Always center the data before PCA. The choice of $M$ is determined by the data: use a scree plot or the 95% explained variance rule as a starting point. When features have different units or scales, standardize before computing $\boldsymbol{S}$ (otherwise PCA will be dominated by high-variance features that may just reflect measurement scale). When $N \ll D$, never form $\boldsymbol{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ explicitly — use the dual formulation (§10.5).

## 10.2 Maximum Variance Perspective

**Principal components** are directions of maximal projected variance. For the $m$-th component:
$$\max_{\boldsymbol{b}_m: \|\boldsymbol{b}_m\|=1,\; \boldsymbol{b}_m \perp \boldsymbol{b}_1,\ldots,\boldsymbol{b}_{m-1}} \boldsymbol{b}_m^T\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{b}_m$$

Via Lagrange multipliers: $\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{b}_m = \lambda_m\boldsymbol{b}_m$. So PCs are eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{S}$, and the variance along $\boldsymbol{b}_m$ is its eigenvalue $\lambda_m$.

**Total variance captured by $M$ components**: $V_M = \sum_{m=1}^M \lambda_m$. Relative explained variance: $V_M / \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{S})$.

> **Intuition.** The eigendecomposition of $\boldsymbol{S}$ rotates the coordinate system into the natural axes of the data distribution — the axes along which the Gaussian approximation to the data is axis-aligned. The largest eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ tells you the variance along the most variable direction $\boldsymbol{b}_1$; the smallest eigenvalue tells you the "thickness" of the data in its flattest direction. PCA is nothing more than a change of basis to this natural coordinate system.

> **Usage.** Explained variance ratio per component is the key diagnostic: if the first 2 PCs explain 90% of variance, a 2D plot faithfully represents the data. The condition number $\lambda_1 / \lambda_D$ reflects data anisotropy — large condition numbers indicate elongated data clouds that benefit greatly from PCA. PCA whitening (dividing each PC by its standard deviation $\sqrt{\lambda_m}$) decorrelates and equalizes variances, often improving downstream learning.

> **Practicality.** Use `sklearn.decomposition.PCA` for standard use. `TruncatedSVD` for sparse matrices (no centering, avoids densification). `IncrementalPCA` for datasets too large to fit in memory. Explained variance ratio is `pca.explained_variance_ratio_`. Scree plot: plot eigenvalues sorted descending, look for an "elbow."

## 10.3 Projection/Reconstruction Perspective

Minimizing average squared reconstruction error:
$$J_M = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \|\boldsymbol{x}_n - \tilde{\boldsymbol{x}}_n\|^2 = \sum_{j=M+1}^D \lambda_j$$

Equivalently, minimizing reconstruction error = maximizing explained variance. The two criteria are dual: one minimizes loss in the orthogonal complement, the other maximizes retention in the principal subspace.

The projection matrix $\boldsymbol{P} = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{B}^T$ is a rank-$M$ approximation to the identity.

> **Intuition.** The reconstruction error equals the total variance of the data projected onto the discarded components. Keeping the top-$M$ eigenvectors minimizes the information lost in the orthogonal complement. This is not an approximation — the Eckart-Young theorem (§10.4) proves this is the *optimal* rank-$M$ linear approximation in Frobenius norm.

> **Usage.** The reconstruction error $J_M$ as a function of $M$ is the PCA "elbow" curve. If you care about reconstruction quality (denoising, compression), plot $J_M$ vs. $M$ and pick the $M$ where further reduction causes unacceptable reconstruction error. For lossy compression, the coding cost of storing $M$ codes plus the reconstruction error is the tradeoff to optimize.

> **Practicality.** PCA denoising: project onto the top $M$ PCs and reconstruct. The discarded components (low-variance directions) are assumed to be noise. This works well when signal and noise live in different subspaces. For images: PCA eigenfaces is an early example — face images reconstructed from top 50–100 PCs are recognizable, demonstrating that faces occupy a low-dimensional subspace of pixel space.

## 10.4 Eigenvector Computation and Low-Rank Approximations

**SVD connection**: $\boldsymbol{X} = \boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{V}^T$ gives $\boldsymbol{S} = \frac{1}{N}\boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2\boldsymbol{U}^T$, so left singular vectors = eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{S}$, and $\lambda_d = \sigma_d^2/N$. In practice, SVD of $\boldsymbol{X}$ is numerically more stable than eigendecomposition of $\boldsymbol{S}$.

**Eckart-Young**: the best rank-$M$ approximation $\hat{\boldsymbol{X}}_M = \boldsymbol{U}_M\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_M\boldsymbol{V}_M^T$ uses the top $M$ singular values. PCA projection matrix $\boldsymbol{B} = \boldsymbol{U}_M$.

**Power iteration**: $\boldsymbol{x}_{k+1} = \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{x}_k / \|\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{x}_k\|$ converges to the dominant eigenvector. Randomized SVD (Halko et al.) scales to large matrices.

> **Intuition.** Computing $\boldsymbol{S}$ first and then its eigendecomposition squares the condition number, amplifying floating-point errors. Computing the SVD of $\boldsymbol{X}$ directly avoids this — the singular values of $\boldsymbol{X}$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $\boldsymbol{S}$, without the squaring step. Numerically, SVD is the preferred route.

> **Usage.** Eckart-Young is the theoretical justification for truncated SVD as matrix approximation: it is used in collaborative filtering (matrix factorization for recommendation), NLP (LSA/LSI), and image compression. The optimality is in Frobenius norm (and also operator norm), making it the gold standard for rank-$M$ approximation.

> **Practicality.** For large matrices, randomized SVD (Halko et al., 2011) computes the top-$M$ SVD in $O(NMD)$ time with high probability by projecting onto a random subspace first. Available as `sklearn.utils.extmath.randomized_svd` or `torch.linalg.svd` with `full_matrices=False`. For streaming/online PCA, incremental SVD updates the decomposition as new data arrives. For very large sparse matrices (text, graphs), use `scipy.sparse.linalg.svds`.

## 10.5 PCA in High Dimensions ($N \ll D$)

Computing $\boldsymbol{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ is $O(ND^2)$ and storing it is $O(D^2)$ — infeasible when $D$ is large. Instead, work with $\boldsymbol{X}^T\boldsymbol{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$:

If $\boldsymbol{X}^T\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{c}_m = N\lambda_m\boldsymbol{c}_m$, then $\boldsymbol{b}_m = \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{c}_m / \|\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{c}_m\|$ is an eigenvector of $\boldsymbol{S}$. Complexity: $O(N^2 D)$ vs $O(D^3)$ — a major saving when $N \ll D$ (e.g., gene expression data, document-term matrices).

> **Intuition.** The covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{S} = \frac{1}{N}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{X}^T$ and the Gram matrix $\boldsymbol{G} = \frac{1}{N}\boldsymbol{X}^T\boldsymbol{X}$ are related by a rank-preserving transformation — they share the same non-zero eigenvalues, and their eigenvectors are related by $\boldsymbol{b}_m = \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{c}_m$. This primal-dual switch (eigendecompose the $N \times N$ Gram matrix instead of the $D \times D$ covariance matrix) is the same move as the kernel trick in SVMs.

> **Usage.** Gene expression data: $N \approx 100$ samples, $D \approx 20{,}000$ genes. Document-term matrices: $N \approx 10^3$ documents, $D \approx 10^5$ vocabulary. Neural network activations: $N \approx$ batch size ($\ll$ hidden dimension). In all these cases, the $N \times N$ Gram matrix is feasible where the $D \times D$ covariance matrix is not.

> **Practicality.** `sklearn.decomposition.PCA` automatically uses the dual SVD when $N < D$ (the `_fit_svd` branch). For kernel PCA (§10.8), the Gram matrix replaces $\boldsymbol{X}^T\boldsymbol{X}$ with the kernel matrix $\boldsymbol{K}_{ij} = k(\boldsymbol{x}_i, \boldsymbol{x}_j)$, enabling nonlinear dimensionality reduction at the cost of $O(N^3)$ eigendecomposition.

## 10.6 PCA in Practice

1. **Center**: subtract empirical mean $\bar{\boldsymbol{x}}$.
2. **Standardize**: divide by per-dimension std $\sigma_d$ (if features have different scales).
3. **Decompose**: SVD of (standardized) data matrix or eigendecomp of sample covariance.
4. **Select $M$**: by explained variance threshold (e.g., 95%) or via scree plot elbow.
5. **Project**: $\boldsymbol{z}_n = \boldsymbol{B}^T(\boldsymbol{x}_n - \bar{\boldsymbol{x}})$. To reconstruct: $\tilde{\boldsymbol{x}}_n = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{z}_n + \bar{\boldsymbol{x}}$.

Standardization matters when features have different units/scales; without it, high-variance features dominate the PCs. If features are naturally comparable (e.g., pixel intensities), standardization may not be needed.

> **Intuition.** Centering is mandatory — PCA computes the covariance matrix of the *centered* data, and an uncentered PCA will find the direction of the mean as the first PC. Standardization is a modeling choice: it makes PCA operate on correlations rather than covariances, treating all features as equally important regardless of scale. This is appropriate for heterogeneous tabular data (height in cm, weight in kg, age in years) but wrong for homogeneous data (pixel values) where scale differences are meaningful.

> **Practicality.** Save the mean $\bar{\boldsymbol{x}}$ and per-dimension stds $\sigma_d$ from the training set — apply the same transform to validation/test data (never fit on test). Store only the top-$M$ loadings $\boldsymbol{B}$ and the mean/std; discard $\boldsymbol{S}$ itself. For new data, the projection $\boldsymbol{z}_* = \boldsymbol{B}^T(\boldsymbol{x}_* - \bar{\boldsymbol{x}})$ is an $O(MD)$ matrix-vector product. The reconstruction error on test data is a useful data quality check: test points with high reconstruction error may be out-of-distribution.

## 10.7 Latent Variable Perspective (Probabilistic PCA)

**PPCA model** (Tipping & Bishop, 1999): $\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{z} + \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, with $\boldsymbol{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{I})$, $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2\boldsymbol{I})$.

Marginal: $p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{B}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \sigma^2) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{B}^T + \sigma^2\boldsymbol{I})$.

MLE solutions: $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\text{ML} = \bar{\boldsymbol{x}}$, $\boldsymbol{B}_\text{ML} = \boldsymbol{T}(\boldsymbol{\Lambda} - \sigma^2\boldsymbol{I})^{1/2}\boldsymbol{R}$ (where $\boldsymbol{T}$ = top-$M$ eigenvectors, $\boldsymbol{R}$ = arbitrary rotation), $\sigma^2_\text{ML} = \frac{1}{D-M}\sum_{j=M+1}^D \lambda_j$ (average unexplained variance).

As $\sigma^2 \to 0$, PPCA recovers standard PCA. Advantages over PCA: principled handling of missing data, Bayesian inference over $M$, generative model for sampling.

> **Intuition.** PPCA is the "noisy" version of PCA: the data are modeled as low-dimensional latent codes $\boldsymbol{z}$ linearly mapped to observation space and corrupted by isotropic Gaussian noise $\sigma^2\boldsymbol{I}$. In the limit $\sigma^2 \to 0$, the noise vanishes and PCA is recovered. The explicit noise model is what enables principled handling of missing data (marginalize over the missing dimensions) and the Bayesian formulation.

> **Usage.** PPCA is used when (a) data has missing entries (marginalize over them in the E-step), (b) you want a generative model for sampling, (c) you want a principled model comparison criterion for choosing $M$ (marginal likelihood), or (d) you want posterior inference over the latent code $\boldsymbol{z}$ (the posterior $p(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x})$ is Gaussian and has a closed form). Factor analysis generalizes PPCA by using a diagonal noise covariance $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ instead of isotropic $\sigma^2\boldsymbol{I}$.

> **Practicality.** PPCA is fit via EM (E-step computes $\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x}]$ and $\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{z}\boldsymbol{z}^T \mid \boldsymbol{x}]$; M-step updates $\boldsymbol{B}$, $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, $\sigma^2$). The EM algorithm for PPCA handles missing data naturally: the E-step uses only observed dimensions of each $\boldsymbol{x}_n$. The arbitrary rotation $\boldsymbol{R}$ means PPCA does not produce a unique $\boldsymbol{B}$; fixing $\boldsymbol{R} = \boldsymbol{I}$ (aligned with eigenvectors) recovers the standard PCA axes.

## 10.8 Connections and Extensions

- **PCA as linear autoencoder**: encoder $\boldsymbol{z} = \boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}$, decoder $\tilde{\boldsymbol{x}} = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{z}$. Nonlinear autoencoders generalize this but lose the closed-form guarantee.
- **PCA as mutual information maximization**: maximizes $I(\boldsymbol{x}; \boldsymbol{z})$ under a Gaussian assumption.
- **Factor Analysis (FA)**: relaxes $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Psi})$ with diagonal $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ (heteroscedastic noise) vs PPCA's isotropic $\sigma^2\boldsymbol{I}$.
- **ICA**: relaxes Gaussian assumption on $\boldsymbol{z}$, enabling recovery of statistically independent sources.
- **Kernel PCA**: replaces linear projections with kernel inner products, enabling nonlinear dimensionality reduction.
- **GP-LVM**: GP prior on the mapping from latent to observed, fully Bayesian nonlinear alternative.

> **Intuition.** PCA is the globally optimal linear autoencoder under MSE loss: the encoder-decoder pair $(\boldsymbol{B}^T, \boldsymbol{B})$ with $\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{B} = \boldsymbol{I}$ that minimizes $\mathbb{E}\|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}\|^2$ is exactly the top-$M$ eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{S}$. Deep autoencoders drop the linearity constraint — they can represent more complex manifolds, but lose the closed-form guarantee and the orthogonality of the latent space.

> **Usage.** FA is preferred over PPCA when features have different noise levels (e.g., sensor data with different measurement uncertainties). ICA is used for blind source separation — the classic example is the cocktail party problem (separating independent audio sources from mixed recordings). Kernel PCA and t-SNE/UMAP are the standard nonlinear alternatives for visualization — kernel PCA has the theoretical PPCA connection; t-SNE and UMAP optimize different objectives better suited for preserving local neighborhood structure.

> **Practicality.** For visualization: t-SNE and UMAP are nearly universally preferred over kernel PCA because they preserve cluster structure better in 2D. For preprocessing before classification: linear PCA or random projections (Johnson-Lindenstrauss) are faster and often sufficient. For generative modeling: VAE is the deep generalization of PPCA — the encoder is $q(\boldsymbol{z} \mid \boldsymbol{x})$ (approximate posterior), the decoder is $p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{z})$, and the ELBO is the training objective.

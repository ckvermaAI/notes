# Vector Calculus

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 5*

---

## 5.1 Differentiation: Univariate

The derivative $\frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$ gives the instantaneous rate of change (slope of the tangent). Standard rules: product, quotient, sum, chain rule.

**Taylor series** approximates $f$ around $x_0$:
$$T_\infty(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}(x - x_0)^k$$
For polynomials of degree $\leq n$, $T_n$ is exact. The second-order expansion $f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{1}{2}f''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2$ is used extensively in optimization (Newton's method, natural gradient).

## 5.2 Partial Derivatives and Gradients

For $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the **gradient** is the row vector of partial derivatives:
$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}} f = \left[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right] \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times n}$$

The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent; its negative is steepest descent.

### Chain Rule (multivariate)
For $f(\boldsymbol{x}(t))$: $\frac{df}{dt} = \sum_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\frac{\partial x_i}{\partial t}$. In matrix form for $f(\boldsymbol{x}(\boldsymbol{s}))$:
$$\frac{df}{d\boldsymbol{s}} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}} \cdot \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial \boldsymbol{s}}$$

**Gradient check**: numerically verify $\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \approx \nabla f(x)$ with small $h$; error $< 10^{-6}$ indicates correctness.

## 5.3 Jacobian for Vector-Valued Functions

For $\boldsymbol{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, the **Jacobian** is:
$$\boldsymbol{J} = \frac{d\boldsymbol{f}}{d\boldsymbol{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$$

- For $\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x}$: $\boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{A}$.
- For the least-squares loss $L = \|\boldsymbol{e}\|^2$, $\boldsymbol{e}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{\Phi}\boldsymbol{\theta}$: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \boldsymbol{e}} \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{e}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}} = 2\boldsymbol{e}^T(-\boldsymbol{\Phi}) = -2(\boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{\Phi}\boldsymbol{\theta})^T\boldsymbol{\Phi}$.

The **Jacobian determinant** $|\det(\boldsymbol{J})|$ gives the local volume scaling factor under a coordinate transformation (crucial in change-of-variables for probability densities).

## 5.4 Gradients of Matrices

Differentiating w.r.t. a matrix yields a tensor. In practice, handled via vectorization or index notation. For $\boldsymbol{f} = \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x}$ w.r.t. $\boldsymbol{A}$: $\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial A_{pq}} = x_q \mathbf{1}[p = i]$, giving a $(M \times MN)$ structured tensor.

---

## 5.5 Useful Gradient Identities

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}(\boldsymbol{x}^T\boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{x}) = \boldsymbol{x}^T(\boldsymbol{B} + \boldsymbol{B}^T) \quad [\text{= } 2\boldsymbol{x}^T\boldsymbol{B} \text{ if } \boldsymbol{B} \text{ is symmetric}]$$
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}}\det(f(\boldsymbol{X})) = \det(f(\boldsymbol{X}))\,\text{tr}\!\left(f(\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\frac{\partial f(\boldsymbol{X})}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}}\right)$$
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}}\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{X}) = \boldsymbol{A}^T$$

These are used repeatedly in deriving MLE/MAP estimators for Gaussians.

## 5.6 Backpropagation and Automatic Differentiation

For a deep network $\boldsymbol{y} = f_K(f_{K-1}(\cdots f_1(\boldsymbol{x}; \boldsymbol{\theta}_1)\cdots; \boldsymbol{\theta}_{K-1}); \boldsymbol{\theta}_K)$ with loss $L$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f_K} \cdot \prod_{j=i+1}^{K-1} \frac{\partial f_{j+1}}{\partial f_j} \cdot \frac{\partial f_{j+1}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_i}$$

Backpropagation is simply the **reverse-mode automatic differentiation** application of the chain rule, accumulating gradients from output to input using the computation graph. It's $O(|\text{graph}|)$ — same cost as one forward pass.

**Computation graph**: intermediate variables $a_i$ decompose complex functions. Forward pass computes $a_i$; backward pass computes $\frac{\partial L}{\partial a_i}$ via chain rule.

## 5.7 Hessian

The **Hessian** $\boldsymbol{H} = \nabla^2 f \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ collects second-order partial derivatives:
$$H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}$$

For $f \in C^2$, $\boldsymbol{H}$ is symmetric (Schwarz's theorem). The Hessian characterizes critical points: positive definite $\Rightarrow$ local min, negative definite $\Rightarrow$ local max, indefinite $\Rightarrow$ saddle point. Used in Newton's method: $\boldsymbol{x}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{x}_t - \boldsymbol{H}^{-1}\nabla f(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$.

---

## 5.8 Multivariate Taylor Series

$$f(\boldsymbol{x}) \approx f(\boldsymbol{x}_0) + \nabla f(\boldsymbol{x}_0)(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_0) + \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^T\boldsymbol{H}(\boldsymbol{x}_0)(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_0) + \ldots$$

The second-order approximation is the foundation for Newton-type optimizers, natural gradient, and Laplace approximation in Bayesian inference.

## 2.1 Systems of Linear Equations

The canonical form $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{b}$ ($\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$) has three solution regimes: no solution (inconsistent), a unique solution, or a subspace of solutions. Geometrically, each equation defines a hyperplane in $\mathbb{R}^n$; the solution set is their intersection.

---

## 2.2 Matrices

### Inverse and Transpose

- $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}$ exists iff $\boldsymbol{A}$ is square and nonsingular ($\det(\boldsymbol{A}) \neq 0$). The inverse is unique.
- Key identities: $(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B})^{-1} = \boldsymbol{B}^{-1}\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}$, $(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{B})^T = \boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{A}^T$, $(\boldsymbol{A}^{-1})^T = (\boldsymbol{A}^T)^{-1}$.
- $(\boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{B})^{-1} \neq \boldsymbol{A}^{-1} + \boldsymbol{B}^{-1}$ in general.

### Symmetric Matrices
A matrix $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is symmetric if $\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{A}^T$. The sum of symmetric matrices is symmetric; their product is not necessarily so.

## 2.3 Solving Systems of Linear Equations

### Particular and General Solution
For $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{b}$: find one particular solution $\boldsymbol{x}_p$, then add the kernel (null space) of $\boldsymbol{A}$:
$$\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{x}_p + \sum_i \lambda_i \boldsymbol{n}_i, \quad \boldsymbol{n}_i \in \ker(\boldsymbol{A})$$

### Row-Echelon Form (REF)
A matrix is in REF if: zero rows are at the bottom; each nonzero row's leading entry (pivot) is strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above. Variables corresponding to pivot columns are **basic**; others are **free**.

**Reduced REF (RREF)**: additionally, every pivot is 1 and the only nonzero entry in its column.

**Gaussian Elimination**: brings $[\boldsymbol{A} \mid \boldsymbol{b}]$ to RREF via elementary row operations (swap, scale, add), enabling direct solution.

### The Minus-1 Trick
For $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ in RREF, augment to $\tilde{\boldsymbol{A}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ by inserting rows $[0, \ldots, -1, \ldots, 0]$ at each non-pivot column. The columns of $\tilde{\boldsymbol{A}}$ containing $-1$ pivots form a basis for $\ker(\boldsymbol{A})$.

## 2.4–2.6 Vector Spaces, Linear Independence, Basis & Rank

- **Vector space**: closed under addition and scalar multiplication, with a zero vector and additive inverses.
- **Linear independence**: $\{\boldsymbol{x}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{x}_k\}$ are linearly independent iff $\sum_i \lambda_i \boldsymbol{x}_i = \mathbf{0}$ implies all $\lambda_i = 0$.
- **Basis**: a minimal spanning set; equivalently, a linearly independent generating set.
- **Dimension**: the cardinality of any basis (invariant for a given space).
- **Rank** of $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: number of linearly independent columns = number of linearly independent rows. $\text{rk}(\boldsymbol{A}) = n$ iff $\boldsymbol{A}$ is full column rank iff $\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A}$ is invertible.

## 2.7 Linear Mappings

### Definition and Representation

A mapping $\Phi: V \to W$ is **linear** if $\Phi(\lambda\boldsymbol{u} + \psi\boldsymbol{v}) = \lambda\Phi(\boldsymbol{u}) + \psi\Phi(\boldsymbol{v})$.

- **Injective**: $\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = \Phi(\boldsymbol{y}) \Rightarrow \boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{y}$.
- **Surjective**: $\Phi(V) = W$.
- **Bijective** (isomorphism): both; inverse exists.

**Theorem 2.17**: Finite-dimensional vector spaces are isomorphic iff $\dim(V) = \dim(W)$.

### Transformation Matrix
For bases $B = (\boldsymbol{b}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{b}_n)$ of $V$ and $C = (\boldsymbol{c}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{c}_m)$ of $W$, the transformation matrix $\boldsymbol{A}_\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ has columns given by coordinates of $\Phi(\boldsymbol{b}_j)$ in $C$:
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{y}} = \boldsymbol{A}_\Phi \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}$$
where $\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{y}}$ are coordinate vectors w.r.t. $B$ and $C$.

### Kernel and Image

$$\ker(\Phi) = \{\boldsymbol{v} \in V \mid \Phi(\boldsymbol{v}) = \mathbf{0}\} \subseteq V \quad \text{(null space of } \boldsymbol{A}_\Phi)$$
$$\text{im}(\Phi) = \{\boldsymbol{w} \in W \mid \exists \boldsymbol{v}: \Phi(\boldsymbol{v}) = \boldsymbol{w}\} \subseteq W \quad \text{(column space of } \boldsymbol{A}_\Phi)$$

**Rank-Nullity Theorem**: $\dim(\ker(\Phi)) + \dim(\text{im}(\Phi)) = \dim(V)$.

This directly implies: $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{b}$ has a solution iff $\boldsymbol{b} \in \text{im}(\boldsymbol{A})$, and the solution is unique iff $\ker(\boldsymbol{A}) = \{\mathbf{0}\}$.

### Composition of Linear Mappings

If $\Phi: U \to V$ and $\Psi: V \to W$, then $\boldsymbol{A}_{\Psi \circ \Phi} = \boldsymbol{A}_\Psi \boldsymbol{A}_\Phi$. Note the reversed order due to non-commutativity of matrix multiplication.

## Other Definitions

**Equivalence** (Definition 2.21): $A, \tilde{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ are equivalent if $\exists$ regular $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $T \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ such that $\tilde{A} = T^{-1}AS$.

**Similarity** (Definition 2.22): $A, \tilde{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ are similar if $\exists$ regular $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ such that $\tilde{A} = S^{-1}AS$. Similar matrices represent the same linear mapping under different bases and share eigenvalues, determinant, and trace.

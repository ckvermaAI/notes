# Analytic Geometry

*Source: Mathematics for Machine Learning, Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong (2020), Chapter 3*

---

## 3.1 Norms

A **norm** $\|\cdot\|: V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies: absolute homogeneity ($\|\lambda\boldsymbol{x}\| = |\lambda|\|\boldsymbol{x}\|$), triangle inequality ($\|\boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{y}\| \leq \|\boldsymbol{x}\|+\|\boldsymbol{y}\|$), positive definiteness ($\|\boldsymbol{x}\| = 0 \Leftrightarrow \boldsymbol{x} = \mathbf{0}$).

- **$\ell_1$ (Manhattan)**: $\|\boldsymbol{x}\|_1 = \sum_i |x_i|$ — promotes sparsity (LASSO, compressed sensing).
- **$\ell_2$ (Euclidean)**: $\|\boldsymbol{x}\|_2 = \sqrt{\boldsymbol{x}^T\boldsymbol{x}}$ — default geometric distance; induced by the dot product inner product.

Not all norms arise from inner products (e.g., $\ell_1$ does not).

---

## 3.2 Inner Products

A **bilinear mapping** $\Omega: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ is linear in each argument. An **inner product** $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is additionally:
- **Symmetric**: $\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \rangle = \langle \boldsymbol{y}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle$
- **Positive definite**: $\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle > 0$ for $\boldsymbol{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$

### Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) Matrices

For a basis $B = (\boldsymbol{b}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{b}_n)$, any inner product takes the form $\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \rangle = \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}^T \boldsymbol{A} \hat{\boldsymbol{y}}$ where $A_{ij} = \langle \boldsymbol{b}_i, \boldsymbol{b}_j \rangle$. This matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$ is SPD iff $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is a valid inner product. SPD matrices arise in Cholesky decomposition, Gaussian covariance matrices, and kernel methods.

## 3.3 Lengths, Distances, Cauchy-Schwarz

An inner product induces a norm: $\|\boldsymbol{x}\| = \sqrt{\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle}$.

**Cauchy-Schwarz**: $|\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \rangle| \leq \|\boldsymbol{x}\| \|\boldsymbol{y}\|$ — equality iff $\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}$ are collinear.

A **metric** $d: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies positive definiteness, symmetry, and triangle inequality. The Euclidean distance $d(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}) = \|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{y}\|_2$ is the canonical example.

## 3.4 Angles and Orthogonality

The angle $\omega \in [0, \pi]$ between nonzero $\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}$:
$$\cos\omega = \frac{\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \rangle}{\|\boldsymbol{x}\|\|\boldsymbol{y}\|}$$

**Orthogonality**: $\boldsymbol{x} \perp \boldsymbol{y}$ iff $\langle \boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \rangle = 0$. The zero vector is orthogonal to all vectors.

**Orthogonal matrix**: $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with $\boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{I}$, i.e., $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1} = \boldsymbol{A}^T$. Orthogonal matrices preserve both lengths and angles — they represent isometries (rotations, reflections).

---

## 3.5–3.6 Orthonormal Bases and Orthogonal Complement

An **orthonormal basis (ONB)**: $\langle \boldsymbol{b}_i, \boldsymbol{b}_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$. In an ONB, coordinates are simply $\langle \boldsymbol{b}_i, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle$ — no matrix inversion needed.

**Gram-Schmidt** orthogonalizes any basis: $\boldsymbol{u}_k = \boldsymbol{b}_k - \sum_{i<k} \frac{\langle \boldsymbol{b}_k, \boldsymbol{u}_i \rangle}{\langle \boldsymbol{u}_i, \boldsymbol{u}_i \rangle} \boldsymbol{u}_i$, then normalize.

For $M$-dimensional $U \subseteq V$ ($\dim V = D$), the **orthogonal complement** $U^\perp$ is $(D-M)$-dimensional and $V = U \oplus U^\perp$ (unique decomposition).

---

## 3.7 Inner Product of Functions

For $u, v: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$: $\langle u, v \rangle = \int_a^b u(x) v(x)\, dx$. This defines orthogonality on function spaces. The collection $\{1, \cos(x), \cos(2x), \ldots\}$ is orthogonal on $[-\pi, \pi]$, forming the basis for Fourier series.

---

## 3.8 Orthogonal Projections

A linear mapping $\pi: V \to U$ is a **projection** if $\pi^2 = \pi$. Its matrix satisfies $\boldsymbol{P}_\pi^2 = \boldsymbol{P}_\pi$.

### Projection onto a Subspace

For $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ with basis matrix $\boldsymbol{B} = [\boldsymbol{b}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{b}_m]$:
$$\pi_U(\boldsymbol{x}) = \boldsymbol{B}(\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{B})^{-1}\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}, \quad \boldsymbol{P}_\pi = \boldsymbol{B}(\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{B})^{-1}\boldsymbol{B}^T$$

The coordinates are $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = (\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{B})^{-1}\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}$ (the **normal equations**, identical to least-squares). The term $(\boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{B})^{-1}\boldsymbol{B}^T$ is the (left) pseudo-inverse of $\boldsymbol{B}$, valid when $\boldsymbol{B}$ has full column rank.

If $B$ is orthonormal, $\boldsymbol{P}_\pi = \boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{B}^T$ and $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \boldsymbol{B}^T\boldsymbol{x}$ — no inversion required.

**Key application**: MLE in linear regression is equivalent to orthogonal projection of $\boldsymbol{y}$ onto the column space of $\boldsymbol{\Phi}$ (see Chapter 9).

## 3.9 Rotations

A rotation in $\mathbb{R}^2$ by $\theta$ counterclockwise:
$$\boldsymbol{R}(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the **Givens rotation** $\boldsymbol{R}_{ij}(\theta)$ rotates in the $i$-$j$ plane (identity elsewhere). Rotations in 3D+ are non-commutative. Rotations are orthogonal matrices with $\det = +1$; reflections are orthogonal with $\det = -1$.

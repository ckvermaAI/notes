## 7.1 Gradient Descent

For $\min_{\boldsymbol{x}} f(\boldsymbol{x})$, $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ differentiable:
$$\boldsymbol{x}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{x}_t - \gamma_t (\nabla f(\boldsymbol{x}_t))^T$$

Gradients are row vectors by convention; the update uses the transpose. For small enough $\gamma_t$, $f(\boldsymbol{x}_{t+1}) \leq f(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$.

**Step size**: too small → slow; too large → divergence/oscillation. Adaptive strategies: reduce $\gamma$ on function increase, increase on decrease. In practice: line search, Armijo condition, or schedule-based (e.g., cosine annealing).

### Momentum

Adds memory of past updates, smoothing oscillations in ill-conditioned problems (elongated loss surfaces):
$$\boldsymbol{x}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{x}_t - \gamma_t (\nabla f(\boldsymbol{x}_t))^T + \alpha \Delta\boldsymbol{x}_t$$

where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ and $\Delta\boldsymbol{x}_t = \boldsymbol{x}_t - \boldsymbol{x}_{t-1}$. Equivalent to a heavy-ball method: the exponential moving average of past gradients. Nesterov momentum adds a lookahead correction for better convergence guarantees.

### Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

Full batch $\nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_n \nabla L_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is $O(N)$ per step. SGD uses a mini-batch subset:
$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \gamma_t \sum_{n \in \mathcal{B}} \nabla L_n(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

The mini-batch gradient is an **unbiased estimator** of the full gradient. Converges under diminishing step-size schedule ($\sum_t \gamma_t = \infty$, $\sum_t \gamma_t^2 < \infty$). Noise in SGD can help escape poor local minima and saddle points; small batches are noisier, large batches approximate full-batch GD.

## 7.2 Constrained Optimization: Lagrange Multipliers

For $\min_{\boldsymbol{x}} f(\boldsymbol{x})$ s.t. $g_i(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq 0$, $h_j(\boldsymbol{x}) = 0$:

**Lagrangian**:
$$\mathfrak{L}(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = f(\boldsymbol{x}) + \sum_i \lambda_i g_i(\boldsymbol{x}) + \sum_j \mu_j h_j(\boldsymbol{x})$$

with $\lambda_i \geq 0$ (inequality) and $\mu_j \in \mathbb{R}$ (equality).

> **Intuition:** The Lagrangian folds constraints into the objective by penalizing violation. The multipliers $\lambda_i, \mu_j$ are prices: at optimality they measure how much the objective would improve if a constraint were relaxed by one unit (shadow prices). Minimizing over $\boldsymbol{x}$ then maximizing over $\boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu}$ recovers the constrained optimum when strong duality holds.

**Dual function**: $\mathfrak{D}(\boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \min_{\boldsymbol{x}} \mathfrak{L}(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu})$. The dual problem $\max_{\boldsymbol{\lambda} \geq 0, \boldsymbol{\mu}} \mathfrak{D}(\boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu})$ is always convex (even if the primal is not).

> **Usage:** Convexity of the dual means it can always be solved reliably regardless of primal structure. This is exploited heavily in SVMs: the non-convex-looking primal margin problem dualizes to a QP solvable with standard solvers, and the dual variables $\alpha_i$ directly identify support vectors.

**Weak duality**: $\mathfrak{D}^* \leq f^*$ (dual optimal $\leq$ primal optimal). **Strong duality** (primal = dual): holds under Slater's condition for convex problems (see §7.3).

> **In practice:** The duality gap $f^* - \mathfrak{D}^*$ serves as a termination certificate in interior-point solvers — you stop when the gap is below a tolerance. Strong duality is what makes the SVM dual not merely a lower bound but an exact reformulation.

**KKT conditions** (necessary at optimality for smooth problems, sufficient for convex):
1. Stationarity: $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}} \mathfrak{L} = \mathbf{0}$
2. Primal feasibility: $g_i(\boldsymbol{x}^*) \leq 0$, $h_j(\boldsymbol{x}^*) = 0$
3. Dual feasibility: $\lambda_i^* \geq 0$
4. Complementary slackness: $\lambda_i^* g_i(\boldsymbol{x}^*) = 0$ — either $\lambda_i = 0$ or the constraint is active.

> **Intuition:** Complementary slackness is the key structural insight: an inequality constraint either binds (active, $g_i = 0$) and carries a positive price $\lambda_i > 0$, or it is slack (inactive) and its multiplier is zero — it contributes nothing to the solution. **In practice:** KKT conditions are used to characterize SVM support vectors ($\alpha_i > 0 \Leftrightarrow$ point lies on the margin), and form the inner loop of active-set and interior-point solvers. Checking KKT residuals is a standard convergence diagnostic.

## 7.3 Convex Optimization

**Convex set**: $\theta\boldsymbol{x} + (1-\theta)\boldsymbol{y} \in \mathcal{C}$ for all $\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y} \in \mathcal{C}$, $\theta \in [0,1]$.

**Convex function**: $f(\theta\boldsymbol{x} + (1-\theta)\boldsymbol{y}) \leq \theta f(\boldsymbol{x}) + (1-\theta)f(\boldsymbol{y})$. Equivalently (for differentiable $f$): $f(\boldsymbol{y}) \geq f(\boldsymbol{x}) + \nabla f(\boldsymbol{x})^T(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{x})$ (first-order characterization); for twice-differentiable: $\nabla^2 f(\boldsymbol{x}) \succeq 0$ (PSD Hessian).

> **Intuition:** The first-order characterization says the tangent hyperplane is a global underestimate — the function always curves upward away from any linearization. The PSD Hessian condition is the practical check: eigenvalues of the Hessian all non-negative means the curvature is non-negative in every direction.

For convex $f$ and constraints, every local minimum is a global minimum. Non-negative weighted sums of convex functions are convex. Composition rules: $g(f(\boldsymbol{x}))$ is convex if $g$ is convex nondecreasing and $f$ is convex.

> **Intuition.** The first-order characterization says the tangent hyperplane is a global underestimate — the function always curves upward away from any linearization. A zero gradient therefore certifies a global minimum. The composition rules are what you use to verify convexity of loss functions without computing the Hessian: cross-entropy is $-\log$ (convex nondecreasing) composed with a linear function, so it's convex. Softplus $\log(1 + e^x)$ is convex; $\log\sum_k e^{x_k}$ (log-sum-exp) is convex — both are building blocks of neural network losses.

> **In practice.** Checking convexity via PSD Hessian works when the function is simple enough to differentiate twice. For composite objectives, use the composition rules. For black-box functions, convexity is not directly verifiable — which is why non-convex deep learning objectives are treated empirically (monitor training curves, compare multiple runs). CVXPY recognizes a disciplined convex programming (DCP) subset of operations and automatically verifies convexity at problem construction time.

### Linear Programming

$\min_{\boldsymbol{x}} \boldsymbol{c}^T\boldsymbol{x}$ s.t. $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} \leq \boldsymbol{b}$. Dual: $\max_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}} -\boldsymbol{b}^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ s.t. $\boldsymbol{c} + \boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \mathbf{0}$, $\boldsymbol{\lambda} \geq \mathbf{0}$. Strong duality holds (LP duality).

> **Intuition.** The feasible set is a polytope (intersection of halfspaces); a linear objective has no curvature so it attains its minimum at a vertex (extreme point) — you can always improve or maintain by moving to a vertex. Simplex walks edges of the polytope from vertex to vertex. The dual variables $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ are shadow prices: $\lambda_i$ tells you how much the optimal cost improves if constraint $i$ is relaxed by one unit.

> **Usage.** LP relaxations of combinatorial problems (round the LP solution to get an approximate integer solution), optimal transport (Wasserstein distance = LP over the coupling polytope), $L_1$-norm minimization via LP reformulation (basis pursuit), MAP inference in Markov random fields (LP relaxation of the integer program). Simplex and interior-point are both $O(n^3)$ worst-case but fast in practice.

> **Practicality.** `scipy.optimize.linprog` for small LPs. `cvxpy` with `cp.Problem(cp.Minimize(c @ x), [A @ x <= b]).solve()` for prototyping. For large-scale or structured LPs (network flow, assignment): use specialized solvers (GLPK, Gurobi, HiGHS). The dual LP is often the more interpretable object — its variables are the marginal values of resources.

### Quadratic Programming

$\min_{\boldsymbol{x}} \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{x}^T\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{x} + \boldsymbol{c}^T\boldsymbol{x}$ s.t. $\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{x} \leq \boldsymbol{b}$, $\boldsymbol{Q} \succ 0$. The SVM primal/dual formulation is a QP (see Chapter 12). Solution via $\boldsymbol{x}^* = -\boldsymbol{Q}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{c} + \boldsymbol{A}^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}^*)$.

> **Intuition.** Adding a PD quadratic term to the linear objective creates a strictly convex bowl — the unique unconstrained minimum is at $\boldsymbol{x}^* = -\boldsymbol{Q}^{-1}\boldsymbol{c}$. Constraints carve out a feasible region; the constrained optimum is the projection of the unconstrained minimum onto the feasible set in the $\boldsymbol{Q}$-metric. The KKT conditions characterize the optimum: active constraints push the solution away from the unconstrained minimum.

> **Usage.** SVM (primal and dual), ridge regression (unconstrained QP, closed-form), portfolio optimization (mean-variance efficient frontier), elastic net via ADMM. Any $L_2$-regularized linear model with inequality constraints is a QP. The SVM dual is a QP with a box constraint ($0 \leq \alpha_n \leq C$) and a linear equality ($\sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0$).

> **Practicality.** SMO (Sequential Minimal Optimization) solves the SVM dual QP by cycling over the smallest possible 2-variable subproblem, which has an exact closed-form solution. This avoids forming or inverting the full Hessian (the $N \times N$ kernel matrix). OSQP is a modern general-purpose QP solver suitable for both sparse and dense problems. For unconstrained ridge regression, the closed form is faster than any iterative solver for $K < 10^4$.

### Legendre-Fenchel (Convex Conjugate)

The convex conjugate of $f$:
$$f^*(\boldsymbol{s}) = \sup_{\boldsymbol{x}} \big(\langle \boldsymbol{s}, \boldsymbol{x} \rangle - f(\boldsymbol{x})\big)$$

Properties: $f^*$ is always convex (even if $f$ is not); $(f^*)^* = f$ for convex $f$ (Fenchel duality). Key use: converting primal problems to dual forms (e.g., SVM, kernel ridge regression). For $f(\boldsymbol{y}) = \frac{\lambda}{2}\boldsymbol{y}^T\boldsymbol{K}^{-1}\boldsymbol{y}$: $f^*(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{1}{2\lambda}\boldsymbol{\alpha}^T\boldsymbol{K}\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ — this is how kernelized ridge regression dual arises.

> **Intuition.** $f^*(\boldsymbol{s})$ is the maximum profit when the price of each unit of $\boldsymbol{x}$ is $\boldsymbol{s}$ and cost is $f(\boldsymbol{x})$. Geometrically: $f^*(\boldsymbol{s})$ encodes $f$ via its support function — the intercept of the supporting hyperplane with slope $\boldsymbol{s}$. The conjugate swaps the roles of primal variable ($\boldsymbol{x}$) and dual variable (slope $\boldsymbol{s}$); this is exactly why it converts weight-space formulations into kernel/feature-space duals. Key conjugate pairs: $\frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{x}\|^2 \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{s}\|^2$ (self-conjugate); $\|\boldsymbol{x}\|_1 \leftrightarrow \mathbb{I}[\|\boldsymbol{s}\|_\infty \leq 1]$; $x\log x \leftrightarrow e^{s-1}$ (entropy ↔ exponential family).

> **Usage.** Deriving the dual of regularized ERM — replace the primal penalty $f(\boldsymbol{w})$ with its conjugate to get a dual over per-sample $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$. This is the canonical route from primal ridge regression to kernelized ridge regression (kernel ridge regression). Bregman divergences $D_f(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{y}) = f(\boldsymbol{x}) - f(\boldsymbol{y}) - \nabla f(\boldsymbol{y})^T(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{y})$ are defined by convex $f$; KL divergence is $D_{x\log x}$, squared error is $D_{\frac{1}{2}\|\cdot\|^2}$.

> **Practicality.** The conjugate is primarily a theoretical tool for deriving duals — you use it once at the whiteboard, not at runtime. The key practical skill is recognizing when a primal problem can be dualized: if the objective is separable in $\boldsymbol{x}$ and the constraints couple $\boldsymbol{x}$ with other variables, dualizing the coupling constraint (e.g., $\boldsymbol{y} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}\boldsymbol{x}$) and applying the conjugate to the separable part yields the dual in $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ — this is the standard derivation of all kernelized methods.
